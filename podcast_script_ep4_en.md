Speaker 1: Welcome back to Retbaa Circle Insider. I'm Alex. Today's episode is a bit different. We're not talking about a market, a number, or a milestone. We're talking about something harder to quantify — and more powerful in the long run. We're talking about community.

Speaker 2: And I'm Kemia. This episode is based on a strategic document Massata commissioned specifically on community-building for Retbaa. It's called "The Unnamed" — and I'd argue it's one of the most important strategic pieces in the entire Retbaa dossier.

Speaker 1: Before we get into Retbaa specifically — let's understand the principle. Why does community matter so much for a luxury brand?

Speaker 2: There's a distinction that Kapferer — Jean-Noël Kapferer, professor at HEC Paris, one of the most respected theorists of luxury brands globally — makes between notoriety and legitimacy. Notoriety is what you get when you spend on advertising. Legitimacy is what you earn when the right people believe in you. And the crucial difference is this: notoriety disappears when the budget stops. Legitimacy compounds over time. It creates a moat that no competitor can purchase. Apple, Nespresso, Aesop, Le Labo — these brands didn't build markets by shouting. They built tribes by whispering to the right people first.

Speaker 1: Let's go through those references. Apple first — because that parallel is striking.

Speaker 2: In 1984, Apple didn't launch a computer. Apple launched a religion. Guy Kawasaki, Apple's first Chief Evangelist — and that word, evangelist, is the key — said it directly: the mission wasn't to sell computers. It was to change the world. The first Macintosh buyers didn't buy a product. They joined a movement. They became evangelists who converted their friends, their colleagues, their families. Apple grew because its first users felt that using a Mac said something profound about who they were. The product was almost secondary.

Speaker 1: Nespresso is interesting because it's a completely different category — coffee — but the mechanic is the same.

Speaker 2: The Nespresso Club is one of the most studied community mechanisms in luxury marketing. The insight was simple: the capsule is the pretext. The real product is membership. And the language matters: Nespresso members don't say "I use Nespresso." They say "I'm a member of the Nespresso Club." That shift from consumption to belonging is everything. It's the difference between a customer and a believer.

Speaker 1: Aesop does something even more radical — they've never advertised.

Speaker 2: Never. Not once. Aesop built its entire community around one ritual: the handwashing experience offered to every visitor to every Aesop store. Free. No obligation to buy. Just the care, the texture, the scent, and a conversation — sometimes about Borges, sometimes about Mishima. The first touchpoint is sensorial and free. What Retbaa can draw from that is direct: entry into the community must be through an experience, not a transaction.

Speaker 1: And Le Labo — the geography angle.

Speaker 2: Le Labo created what they call City Exclusives — fragrances available only in one city. You travel to New York for the Rose 31. You go to Amsterdam for the Bergamote 22. The community is built around a shared secret: knowing which fragrance belongs to which city. Le Labo fans don't need badges or loyalty points. They recognize each other by a scent and a philosophy. The document proposes Retbaa do something similar — anchor specific experiences to specific cities. An event in Dakar, a tasting in Paris, a ritual in Dubai. Each city becomes a point of pilgrimage.

Speaker 1: So what is "The Unnamed" exactly — as a concept for Retbaa?

Speaker 2: It comes directly from Retbaa's founding manifesto: "Name the Unnamed." The premise is that Africa's extraordinary raw materials — the pink salt of the Lac Rose, the pepper of Penja, the vanilla of Madagascar — have fed the supply chains of European luxury brands for generations, anonymously, without credit or recognition. Retbaa's mission is to name them. To give them authorship. The Unnamed, as a community concept, inverts this beautifully: if Retbaa names what was invisible, its ambassadors are the ones who name Retbaa — who make it visible in the world — before anyone else does.

Speaker 1: Who are these ambassadors? The document identifies five specific profiles.

Speaker 2: Five archetypes. First: the cultivated African diaspora. Thirty to fifty years old, based in Paris, London, New York, Dubai, Montreal. Executives, entrepreneurs, doctors, lawyers. Proud of their origins, deeply critical of brands that perform African identity without embodying it. For them, buying Retbaa is a political act as much as an aesthetic one. Second: the European cultural curious. Urban, slow-life oriented, reads Monocle and The Gentlewoman, shops at Merci. They want traceability, authenticity, the story behind the object. Retbaa's verified supply chain — its blockchain traceability — is exactly what earns their trust.

Speaker 1: And the third archetype?

Speaker 2: The luxury experience prescriber. Journalists, creative directors, starred chefs, sommeliers, luxury hoteliers. Their word has weight. When a Michelin-starred chef puts Penja pepper on their menu, they don't make a concession — they elevate their level. The document proposes approaching these people not with a press release, but with a hand-signed letter from Massata and an invitation to a private backstage tasting. Never a generic message. Always personal.

Speaker 1: The fourth and fifth?

Speaker 2: Fourth: the impact investor and African entrepreneur. People who are building a thesis that African excellence is a scalable, investable market. Retbaa is proof of concept for that thesis — two gold medals at the Pentawards, the world's most rigorous packaging competition, first African brand ever to reach that level. They want evidence. Retbaa is the evidence. Fifth: the niche credibility influencer. Not mega-influencers with millions of followers and 0.5% engagement. Creators with twenty to five hundred thousand followers, above 4% engagement, communities that trust them like a knowledgeable friend. Retbaa is visually extraordinary — the pink lake, the spices in macro, the candle flame — it creates itself in the right hands.

Speaker 1: How does the community actually get built? What's the concrete plan?

Speaker 2: Three phases. Phase one is "The Spark." The objective: one hundred founding ambassadors, selected by hand — not through a form, not through automation. Each person receives what the document calls "The Unnamed Box." A coffret with one product from each of Retbaa's three universes: a Brise de Dakar candle, a pouch of fleur de sel from the Lac Rose, and a pure shea butter soap. Plus a parchment with the "Name the Unnamed" manifesto printed in exclusive typography. Plus — and this is the detail that matters — a personalized video from Massata himself explaining why this specific person was chosen. Not a template. A real message.

Speaker 1: And what's asked of them in return?

Speaker 2: Nothing mandatory. Never. The document is emphatic: you don't ask ambassadors to post. You inspire them to. If they feel moved to share, you give them the tools — exclusive visuals, the story, the history. But the relationship starts with generosity, not transaction. And then: a private WhatsApp group. The Unnamed space. Where Retbaa shares early news, behind-the-scenes access, open questions. The intimacy of WhatsApp, not the performance of Instagram.

Speaker 1: Phase two is the events.

Speaker 2: "The Odyssey Sensorial" — private evenings of thirty to fifty people in three cities: Paris, Dubai, Abidjan. The format is precise: olfactory welcome with Retbaa candles, a guided blind tasting of the spices, a hands-on shea butter moment, ten minutes of Massata speaking about the history and the vision, then a cocktail infused with Retbaa spices. Each event is an immersive experience before it's a sales event. And then comes the co-creation program: five ambassadors invited to help develop a new product. Their name appears on the limited-edition packaging. Not symbolic. Real co-ownership of the object.

Speaker 1: And phase three is scale.

Speaker 2: "The Fire." The goal: a thousand active ambassadors in the right cities. A referral program where each Unnamed can sponsor up to three new members per quarter — and sponsoring is a solemn act, you only recommend someone you truly believe in. The new member receives their own Unnamed Box with the sponsor's name on it. And crucially: when Retbaa opens its first pop-up, the Unnamed are already there. The document says this beautifully — they don't attend the opening. They carry it.

Speaker 1: What's the metric of success that Massata is watching?

Speaker 2: Not the number of followers. Not the impressions. One question: are people talking about Retbaa spontaneously to people who don't know it yet? When the answer is yes, the strategy has worked. The rest — the press, the distribution, the valuation — follows from that.

Speaker 1: One final thought — why does this matter for investors specifically?

Speaker 2: Because brands with genuine communities don't need to spend like brands without them. Every euro Retbaa doesn't spend on paid acquisition is a euro that improves the margin. And a brand that's carried by believers is a brand that's extraordinarily hard to compete with — you can't replicate a community the way you can replicate a product. The Unnamed strategy is ultimately a moat-building exercise. It builds the thing that can't be bought.

Speaker 1: Thank you, Kemia. Next episode — the Go board. Why Retbaa's strategy looks nothing like the competition's, and why that's exactly right. See you then.

Speaker 2: See you then.
