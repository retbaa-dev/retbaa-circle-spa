Speaker 1: Welcome back to Retbaa Circle Insider. I'm Alex. Today we're stepping back from the details — the quarterly numbers, the flagship, the community program — and looking at the whole board. Because there's a way of understanding Retbaa's strategy that changes how you see everything else. And it starts with a game.

Speaker 2: And I'm Kemia. This episode is based directly on a strategic note I wrote for Massata in April 2026 — it's called "Retbaa Plays Go." I want to be transparent about that: this is my analysis, my reading of the strategy. But it's grounded entirely in what the company is actually doing and the documents it has produced.

Speaker 1: So — the game of Go. For listeners who don't know it: what is Go, and why does it matter here?

Speaker 2: Go is a board game that originated in China over 2,500 years ago. It's the most complex strategy game ever devised — more possible positions than there are atoms in the observable universe. But the principle is simple, and that's what makes it profound. In chess, you win by destroying the opponent's king. In Go, you win by occupying territory. You place stones on a board. You build presence. You encircle. You don't attack frontally — you make the opponent's position irrelevant by being everywhere they're not. The world's most powerful AI systems — including Google's AlphaGo — took decades longer to master Go than chess, precisely because Go requires strategic thinking across the entire board simultaneously, not just tactical depth.

Speaker 1: And Massata plays Go.

Speaker 2: Massata plays Go. This isn't a metaphor I invented — it's a reading of what Retbaa is actually doing. Let me go through the five principles of Go strategy and show you how they map to Retbaa's moves.

Speaker 1: First principle.

Speaker 2: Encircle the empty spaces. The luxury African sensory market doesn't exist as an established category. That's not a problem — it's the opportunity. Rather than attacking LVMH on their terrain, rather than competing with Diptyque for shelf space in Marionnaud, Retbaa traces the contours of a territory that nobody has named yet. Sixty-five SKUs across three universes — Atmosphere, Gourmet, and the Kemia beauty line — distributed through carefully selected partners whose selectivity is itself a signal of value: Le Bon Marché, Galeries Lafayette. There is no frontal battle. There is a presence where no one else has gone.

Speaker 1: Second principle.

Speaker 2: Time as a resource. In 2025, Retbaa generated 191,000 euros in revenue with a 22.7% EBITDA. That's not a model under construction — it's a model that breathes. The Tranche 1 close at 360,000 euros in February 2026 — at 13% dilution — wasn't about buying growth at any cost. It was about preparing the terrain. Massata retains 86.96% of the capital. That's not stubbornness. That's the conviction that time, well managed, is the most powerful lever available. Good Go players don't rush. They let conditions ripen.

Speaker 1: Third principle.

Speaker 2: Letting conditions ripen. The Dubai Mall contract with Galeries Lafayette Dubai Mall is signed. The deployment has been delayed. An impatient investor sees a setback. A Go player sees a stone placed on the board, in the right position, waiting for the right moment to become territory. In Go, a stone played too early can die. A stone played at the right moment structures the entire board. The Dubai Mall presence isn't a bet. It's a position acquired. When the operational, logistical, and market conditions align — and the roadmap anticipates this in 2026-2027 — that stone activates.

Speaker 1: Fourth principle.

Speaker 2: Coexistence. Retbaa doesn't seek to dominate the value chain. It seeks to be indispensable within it — without crushing its partners, without being absorbed by them. The 800,000 euro B2B pipeline under conversion is built on partnerships with players whose legitimacy reinforces Retbaa's. Every distribution point, every reseller, every collaborator is a territory partner. In Go, individual stones don't win. They win as a network. A stone surrounded by allies becomes invulnerable. A stone alone, however well-placed, is vulnerable.

Speaker 1: Fifth principle.

Speaker 2: One mind, many eyes. Massata leads alone. Not out of ego — out of coherence. The sensory vision, the African identity, the aesthetic rigor — these things can't be delegated without dilution. But he sees through a network of twelve AI agents operating in parallel — sourcing, finance, operations, communication, strategic memory. It's a Go player's organization: one central intelligence, eyes everywhere. Efficiency without dilution of the founding vision.

Speaker 1: Let me push back on something. Critics of this approach would say: encircling empty space sounds elegant, but it might mean you're building something nobody wants. What's the counter-argument?

Speaker 2: The Pentawards. Two gold medals and a special jury prize in 2024 — the world's most rigorous packaging design competition. First African brand in the history of the contest. That's not a jury of friends. That's a jury of professionals from the global luxury industry who evaluated Retbaa's work against the best in the world and said: this is excellent. You can't fabricate that. The empty space wasn't empty because nobody wanted it — it was empty because nobody had the clarity and the execution to occupy it credibly. Retbaa does.

Speaker 1: There's another strategic document in the Retbaa archives — written by a contributor named Orion — about empty spaces in luxury. What does that analysis add?

Speaker 2: The Orion analysis makes a structural argument: in any established market, there are what he calls "structural vacuums" — positions that should exist given consumer demand, but that no brand has claimed because they require a specific combination of cultural authenticity and technical execution that incumbents can't replicate. The Orion framework identifies the African luxury sensory space as precisely one of these vacuums. The demand is there — affluent African consumers, the diaspora, the cultural moment. The execution has historically been absent. Retbaa is the first brand to bring both simultaneously.

Speaker 1: And there's a third document — by a contributor called Solin — about B2B encirclement strategy.

Speaker 2: The Solin note is about how Retbaa approaches corporate accounts — the B2B segment that represents the majority of current revenue. The insight is that Retbaa doesn't sell to corporations. It encircles them. It enters through one contact — a human resources director who needs year-end gifts, a hotel concierge who recommends the spa products, a chief executive who discovers Retbaa at an event. And from that first point of contact, the relationship expands — into other departments, other occasions, other products. The encirclement logic means that once Retbaa is inside a large institution, it becomes very hard to remove. It's embedded in the culture of the client.

Speaker 1: How should investors read all of this — the Go board, the empty spaces, the coexistence?

Speaker 2: It tells you what kind of company you've invested in. You've invested in a company that thinks in decades, not quarters. A company that chooses the right position over the fast move. A company whose founder understands that the most durable luxury brands — Hermès, Aesop, Le Labo — were built by people who refused to rush. That's not reassuring in a short-term way — it's reassuring in a structural way. The Go strategy means that when Retbaa's positions mature — Dubai Mall, the Abidjan flagship, the community of Unnamed ambassadors — they won't just generate revenue. They'll generate territory. And territory, in luxury, is the only thing that compounds forever.

Speaker 1: One final question. The big houses — LVMH, Kering — they could theoretically see the same board and try to occupy the same empty spaces.

Speaker 2: They could try. But here's the Go principle that protects Retbaa: you can't place a stone where another stone already sits. Authenticity is a stone. Massata Niang being African, Retbaa being born in Dakar, the Lac Rose being a real place with a real story — these are stones that were placed long before anyone was watching. LVMH can come to Dakar for a photo shoot. They cannot become Retbaa. The board already has those stones on it. The territory is already being claimed.
